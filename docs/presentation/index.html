Making the Intangible Tangible

Name it · Measure it · Optimise the outcome.

Turning a route-planning judgement into options you can compare — and a choice you can defend.

One promise for the whole talk: if we measure risk, choosing a route stops being a gut call and becomes something we can compute, compare, and justify. Keep it to a sentence and move on.

The bottom line

Name and measure what matters — here, five risks — and route-planning becomes an optimisation problem. For you, that buys four things:

🛡 Defensible

Every route shows why it was chosen — auditable and repeatable.

⑂ Options

Several genuinely different courses of action — not one black box.

🎚 Control

Your priorities drive the result. The maths serves judgement.

⚡ Speed & consistency

Seconds, not hours — and the same answer every time.

The problem we're solving

Move a team from A to B across ~50 km of country. Not all ground is equal — some is dangerous. There are countless routes.

Proof ① — you stay in control

How much each risk matters is your call: a dial per risk. Low = avoid it; high = tolerate it. The maths serves your judgement, it doesn't replace it.

Drag a dial. Tolerate the bandits and the route takes the shortcut through the town; avoid them and it swings well clear.

The Control hero. The default plan already keeps clear of the purple bandit town. Drag "Bandits" tolerance UP — the route opens the shortcut straight through the town to save distance; drag it back down and it swings wide again. Then try "Heat" or "Thirst" to steer it around the red/teal south-east. Message: the decision-maker drives the plan; the tool just obeys their priorities. This fills the Control badge.

One number per step

Combine the dialled risks (plus a little for distance) into a single cost to cross each patch. A route's cost is just the sum of its steps.

  Each bar is one step's cost, coloured by which risks drove it.

Keep the formula OFF the slide. For your own reference: per cell, cost = Σ riskᵢ × (1 − appetiteᵢ) × 10, plus distance × 1 per km. The charts show the risk part only (distance is a flat per-step constant, so it adds no per-cell signal). Point: a whole route collapses to one comparable number.

Proof ② — fast & consistent

With a cost on every step, "find the cheapest route" is a solved problem. The computer weighs thousands of paths in a blink —

 ·  same inputs → same answer, every time.  

The Speed & consistency hero (supporting). The shaded cells are paths the search considered; it floods outward then snaps to the cheapest line. Contrast with hours of manual map-reading, and stress determinism: identical inputs always reproduce the identical plan — vital for audit and for trusting it. Fills the Speed badge.

Proof ③ — options, not one answer

We deliberately surface three genuinely different routes — then you choose the one that fits the day.

Same map, same metrics — the planner's best route plus two real alternatives. The decision stays yours.

The Options hero. The planner doesn't hand over one answer — it surfaces its best (lowest-risk) route plus two genuinely different alternatives, kept distinct on purpose. The alternatives cost a little more risk, but a planner might prefer one for reasons the map can't see (timing, intelligence, orders). The mini-maps show three different lines; the bars and totals show what each costs. The choice stays human. Fills the Options badge.

Proof ④ — defensible by construction ⭐

Every route carries its reasons: the cost, broken down by risk, step by step. Click any step to see why.

The lead payoff, given the climax. Click a step on the route or a bar — it highlights and shows exactly which risks cost what. Then tick "compare with the bolder option": the faster route overlays (dashed) and you see precisely how much extra risk it trades for the distance saved. That is defensibility — you can always answer "why this and not that?". Fills the Defensible badge (the red one).
